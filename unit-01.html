1.3 Accordion · Concept Blocks

Three types of AI you will meet in healthcare education

Not all AI systems work the same way. Knowing which type you are working with shapes every design decision that follows.

Systems that produce new content such as text, images, code, or audio in response to a prompt. Large language models like GPT-4 and Claude sit here. These are the tools faculty most often reach for when AI becomes a verb in a sentence.
Clinical Education Example A nursing faculty member asks a chatbot to draft three case-based scenarios for a cardiac unit discussion board. The AI generates the cases. The faculty member verifies clinical accuracy and adapts for learner level.
Systems that classify, rank, or detect patterns in existing data. They answer "what is this?" rather than "make me this." Plagiarism checkers, AI-text detectors, and radiology triage tools all belong to this family.
Clinical Education Example A pharmacy course uses a discriminative system to flag drug-interaction errors in student SOAP notes. The tool classifies each answer; the instructor reviews flagged items and delivers feedback.
Systems that pair generation with a curated source library, grounding outputs in specific documents. Think of a chatbot that only answers from your course textbook or your clinical protocol binder. This is where much of healthcare's responsible AI adoption is moving.
Clinical Education Example A medical education team builds a retrieval-augmented study partner grounded in the institution's USMLE prep library. Learners ask clinical questions; the tool answers from verified sources with citations, reducing confabulation risk.
