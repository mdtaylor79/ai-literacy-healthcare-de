5.1 Statement A · Unit Welcome

Feedback is teaching presence

Before a rubric row, before any AI workflow, one reframe. Feedback is not correction. Feedback is how teaching presence reaches learners who will never be in the same room as you.

05 · UNIT OPENING
In a DE course, feedback is not the part that comes after teaching. Feedback is the teaching.

In a face-to-face course, teaching presence happens in the room: live questions, live explanations, the body language of a raised eyebrow. In asynchronous DE, the analogous channel is your written feedback on learner work. A comment in the margin is the closest equivalent to the raised eyebrow.

That is also why AI enters the assessment conversation so carefully. An AI tool can extend your reach and your throughput, but only you carry the authority of the course and the discipline. This unit is the last fifteen feet of that reasoning. Three appropriate uses. One firm non-use. A rubric row you can ship.

5.2 Accordion · Three Uses, One Non-Use

Three ways AI helps. One way it cannot.

The boundaries first. Three uses where AI earns its place in the feedback loop. One category where AI must not take the final call, regardless of how well it performs in pilot.

APPROPRIATE USE · 01
✓

Rubric drafting

SAMR · AUGMENTATION

AI drafts a first-pass rubric from a learning outcome. Faculty edits for course-specific criteria, language, and level distinctions. Final rubric remains a human authorship decision.

APPROPRIATE USE · 02
✓

Formative feedback

SAMR · MODIFICATION

AI drafts a first-pass formative comment against your rubric. Faculty reviews, edits, and delivers. Learners receive faster and more consistent feedback than asynchronous teaching alone can produce.

APPROPRIATE USE · 03
✓

Test item review

SAMR · AUGMENTATION

AI reviews draft test items for ambiguity, clinical accuracy, and bias. Faculty adopts, rejects, or revises each suggestion. Item bank remains under human editorial control.

✕
THE NON-USE · DO NOT CROSS

High-stakes final scoring

AI must not serve as the final scoring authority on high-stakes assessments. Midterms, finals, capstones, licensure-adjacent exams, clinical reasoning competency decisions — these require human judgment as the last call, regardless of how well an AI tool performs in validation. An AI that scores well against human graders in a pilot is still not the appropriate final authority on a student's record.

Draft rubric

The instructor establishes the rubric and the outcome alignment before any AI touches the workflow.
The rubric is the contract between teacher and learner. Without it, AI augmentation drifts toward whatever the model finds plausible. With it, every AI suggestion can be evaluated against a stable criterion.
WHAT THE INSTRUCTOR DOES
  • Writes the rubric or adapts an existing one to this assignment.
  • Confirms alignment to a named learning outcome.
  • Decides where AI will augment and where it will not.
ARTIFACT FROM THIS STEP A finalized rubric with criteria, four performance levels, an AI-use expectation row, and a named learning outcome alignment.

5.6 Labeled Graphic · Rubric Row Anatomy

Anatomy of an AI-aware rubric row

One real rubric row, annotated. Hover or click any passage to see the element it represents. Use this as the pattern for your own row in Block 5.7.

WORKED EXAMPLE · CLINICAL REFLECTION CRITERION
"Clinical Reasoning" rubric row with explicit AI-use expectation
A rubric row that holds across four performance levels AND declares what AI use is in-bounds, what requires disclosure, and what crosses into the student's own work. Without the AI-use row, the rubric leaves enforcement to guesswork.
Criterion Exceeds (4) Mastery (3) Near (2) Below (1)
Clinical reasoningLO-4.2 EXCEEDS Integrates multiple evidence sources, acknowledges uncertainty, and defends a clinically-defensible recommendation that accounts for patient context. MASTERY Cites at least one authoritative guideline, arrives at a clinically-defensible recommendation, and shows awareness of at least one alternative. NEAR Recommendation is plausible but reasoning is thin; missing guideline reference or key clinical consideration. BELOW Recommendation is unsafe, unsupported, or not anchored to the clinical case.
AI-USE EXPECTATION
Permitted: AI for brainstorming structure, outlining, and revising clarity. Requires disclosure: AI used to draft any portion of the reasoning (tool, prompt, and scope noted at end of submission). Not permitted: AI as the author of the clinical recommendation itself — the reasoning must be the student's.
ELEMENT 01 •
Criterion tied to outcome
LO anchor. Every rubric row opens with a criterion that maps to a named learning outcome. No outcome, no row.
ELEMENT 02 4
Exceeds
Beyond mastery. Describes performance that integrates, defends, or extends. Should be achievable, not abstract excellence.
ELEMENT 03 3
Mastery
The bar. This is the level that represents the outcome met. Write this level first, then derive the others from it.
ELEMENT 04 2
Near
Specific deficit. Name the exact element missing from Mastery. Avoid adjective-swapping like "less clear" or "somewhat weaker."
ELEMENT 05 1
Below
Unambiguous. The level should be concrete enough that any instructor applying the rubric would agree when it applies.
ELEMENT 06 AI
AI-use expectation
The unit's signature move. Explicitly names permitted, disclosure-required, and not-permitted AI use against this criterion.
