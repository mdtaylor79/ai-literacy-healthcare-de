AI Transparency Note

How AI contributed to this course

A course about AI literacy in healthcare education owes its learners the same disclosure it asks them to make about their own work. This is that disclosure.

This course was authored by Marcus "MD" Taylor, Instructional Designer and AI Integration Specialist at the UNT Health Science Center. The instructional design, the theoretical grounding, the pedagogical sequencing, the clinical scenarios, and every final editorial decision are the author's own. AI tools were used at specific points in the production process, in specific roles. Those roles are named below.

The principle in Unit 03 of this course — AI refines, it does not replace — applies to the course's own production. What AI assisted with is named. What it did not author is also named.

Section 01 · Tool ContributionsWhere AI tools were used

Four AI tools contributed to this course at four distinct stages of production. Each played a bounded role under human editorial control.

Claude
Structure & Layout
Role: HTML structure and visual layout of the five interactive course units. Claude generated the Rise 360-compatible block templates, the editorial grid system, the color-token architecture, and the scroll-spy navigation. Every design decision was reviewed and approved by the author before commit.
Elicit
Research Support
Role: Literature discovery and synthesis for the theoretical grounding. Elicit supported the search across Community of Inquiry, TAM, SRL, and SAMR framework literature. All citations referenced in the course spec were independently verified against primary sources before inclusion.
ChatGPT
Brainstorming
Role: Early-stage idea generation for activity patterns, scenario framings, and block-level content structures. ChatGPT output was used as a starting point for the author's own thinking, never as final content. All ideas adopted into the course were rewritten and refined by the author.
Claude
Redundancy & QA
Role: Redundancy checks across the five units, HTML inconsistency detection, accessibility audits (WCAG 2.1 AA contrast and font-size verification), and cross-reference validation. Claude flagged issues; the author reviewed each flag and decided what to resolve.

Section 02 · Human AuthorshipWhat AI did not do

The following work is entirely the author's and was not delegated to AI at any stage:

The pedagogical argument, the clinical scenarios, the ethical stance, the framework synthesis, the voice, and every final editorial decision are the author's work. AI tools assisted with production. They did not author the course.

This is the same standard the course asks its learners to hold: AI may assist; the human must remain the author of the reasoning, the clinical judgment, and the final artifact.

Authorship Statement
AI refines. It does not replace. The course teaches this. The course was produced under this.

Every AI contribution to this course was bounded, disclosed, and editorially reviewed. The course's framework, its pedagogical sequencing, its scenarios, and its final form are Marcus "MD" Taylor's work. AI tools accelerated production. They did not author the course.

If any passage, citation, or claim in this course appears to be AI-generated without this disclosure, the author requests to be notified so the disclosure can be corrected. Transparency is the practice, not the performance.
