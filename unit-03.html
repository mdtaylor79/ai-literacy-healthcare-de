3.1 Statement A · Stakes

Three stakes no other domain replicates

Healthcare distance education sits at an intersection other disciplines do not. Before a single AI tool touches a patient case, three stakes are already live in the room.

STAKE 01

Clinical accuracy

An AI error in a case study propagates into learner reasoning, then into clinical practice. A fabricated medication dose is not a typo; it is a potential adverse event in training.

STAKE 02

Patient privacy

Real patient data in a prompt is a HIPAA violation in motion. De-identification that feels obvious to a clinician may still carry signature fields a model can recombine.

STAKE 03

Student equity

AI policies that are unclear, inconsistent, or inequitably enforced harm learners unevenly. Disclosure is not a syllabus formality. It is a condition for trust.

3.2 Accordion · Three Failure Modes

Three ways AI fails in healthcare education

Generative AI fails in predictable categories. Learn the three that matter most in clinical contexts. Each one carries a specific signature and a specific mitigation.

Generative models produce outputs that are plausible but unsupported. The model does not know what it does not know. Fabricated citations, invented drug dosages, and cited guidelines that do not exist are the most common patterns in clinical contexts.

Mitigation: every AI-generated claim tied to a clinical fact, a citation, or a guideline must be verified against a primary source before it enters learner-facing material.
Clinical Education Case A faculty member asks an AI tool for three recent articles on sepsis bundles. Two references return with plausible author names, journals, DOIs, and year ranges. One does not exist. The faculty member nearly adds it to a reading list before a librarian catches it. The cost of catching it later: undermined credibility with an entire cohort.
Models reflect the distributions in their training data. In healthcare, that means known patterns: underrepresentation of certain populations in clinical literature, stereotyped symptom presentations, and diagnostic bias documented across specialties.

Mitigation: prompt with explicit demographic variance, audit outputs for stereotyping before deployment, and pair AI-generated cases with intentional counter-examples that represent your full learner community.
Clinical Education Case A cardiology instructor asks for five case vignettes. All five patients present with textbook male-pattern chest pain. The model has reproduced a well-documented gender bias in training data. Deployed as-is, the cases would reinforce the same diagnostic blind spot the course is meant to counteract.
Inputs to a model may be retained for training, cached, or recombined in later outputs. Faculty who paste real patient narratives, de-identified case notes that carry signatures, or student work containing identifiable detail are creating downstream exposure.

Mitigation: use only models with documented no-retain policies for instructional content. Strip identifiers before any clinical narrative enters a prompt. Treat consumer-grade AI tools as unsafe for any content bound by FERPA, HIPAA, or institutional confidentiality policies.
Clinical Education Case A nursing faculty member pastes a de-identified discharge summary into a chatbot to generate a teaching case. The summary still contains the admitting hospital, the unit, and the date of discharge. Combined with the narrative, those fields re-identify the patient to anyone who knows the institution. The chatbot provider retains the input by default.

3.4 Process · The PRISM Ethics Screen

PRISM: five questions, one screen

PRISM is the screen you run any AI-assisted instructional decision through before it reaches a learner. Click each letter to see the dimension, its core questions, and what triggers a stop.

THE SCREEN · PRIVACY · REPRESENTATION · INTEGRITY · SAFETY · MINIMIZATION
Any AI use that cannot pass all five questions is not ready to deploy.

PRISM is designed to be fast: a clinician-educator should be able to run it in under two minutes for a routine use and still catch the decisions that matter.

It is not exhaustive. It is a first filter. A "yes" on every dimension means you can move forward. A "no" or "uncertain" on any dimension means you pause, document, or escalate.

Privacy

Does any input or output contain identifiable patient, student, or institutional data?
Privacy is the dimension most often violated accidentally. De-identified does not mean anonymous; a combination of fields can re-identify. The default stance: nothing protected goes into a model you do not control.
CORE QUESTIONS · ASK BEFORE YOU DEPLOY
  • Does the prompt contain PHI, de-identified or otherwise?
  • Does the prompt contain identifiable student work?
  • Does the tool retain inputs for training?
  • Is there an institutional DPA or BAA covering this tool?
STOP SIGNAL Any uncertainty on retention policies. Any input that could re-identify a patient when combined with other available data.

3.7 Labeled Graphic · Disclosure Examples

Three syllabus disclosures, annotated

Most syllabus AI statements are not wrong — they are incomplete. Three real-style examples below. Green marks a strength. Rust marks a risk. Click either the passage or the note to highlight the pair.

SYLLABUS LANGUAGE · RESTRICTIVE STANCE
Artificial Intelligence Policy

The use of any artificial intelligence tool is strictly prohibited in this course. This includes but is not limited to generative AI such as ChatGPT, Claude, and Gemini. Any violation will be treated as academic dishonesty and referred to the Office of Student Conduct.

Students are expected to complete all work using only course materials and approved references. No exceptions will be made.

ANNOTATIONS
STRENGTH · CLEAR CONSEQUENCE Names the specific consequence and the office involved. Learners know what enforcement looks like.
RISK · BLANKET PROHIBITION A total prohibition cannot survive contact with how students already use AI tools (grammar, translation, search). Enforcement becomes arbitrary.
RISK · NO EXCEPTIONS Absolute language closes off reasonable accommodations (disability services, language learners, legitimate edge cases).
Strength Risk
SYLLABUS LANGUAGE · PERMISSIVE STANCE
AI Use in This Course

Students are encouraged to use AI tools as learning partners. AI can help you brainstorm, draft, refine, and understand course material. You do not need to disclose when or how you used AI in your work.

The instructor trusts students to use AI ethically and productively. There are no restricted uses.

ANNOTATIONS
STRENGTH · LEARNING-PARTNER FRAME Positions AI as a tool that can extend learning. The framing is accurate and empowering.
RISK · NO DISCLOSURE EXPECTATION Without disclosure, faculty cannot assess what the student can do independently. This also conflicts with most accreditor guidance and professional body expectations.
RISK · NO RESTRICTED USES High-stakes work — exams, clinical reasoning assignments, capstones — typically should restrict AI. A permissive stance without carve-outs leaves these unprotected.
Strength Risk
SYLLABUS LANGUAGE · BALANCED STANCE
AI Tools: What Is Permitted, What Is Not

AI tools may support your learning in specific ways. Permitted uses: brainstorming, outlining, grammar support, translation, and understanding difficult readings. Restricted uses: substantive drafting of graded work, final examinations, and clinical reasoning assignments.

For permitted uses, include a one-line AI disclosure at the end of any submission that used an AI tool. Format: "AI tool [name] was used for [purpose]. Prompt: [brief summary]."

If you are uncertain whether a use is permitted, ask me before you submit. See the institutional AI policy at [link] for the full framework.

ANNOTATIONS
STRENGTH · NAMED PERMITTED USES Lists the uses that are in-bounds. Learners can proceed with confidence.
STRENGTH · NAMED RESTRICTED USES Protects high-stakes and clinical reasoning work without a blanket ban.
STRENGTH · CONCRETE DISCLOSURE FORMAT Gives students a disclosure format they can actually use. Removes ambiguity.
STRENGTH · OPEN CHANNEL Invites learners to ask. Ambiguity is normalized, not punished.
Strength Risk
