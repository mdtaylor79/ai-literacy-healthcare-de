4.2 Text on Image · Synthesis

Three interactions, three presences, one activity

Every DE activity sits at the intersection of two frameworks you already know. Learning to read an activity through both lenses is the core move of this unit.

An activity is strong when every interaction activates a presence.

MOORE · HILLMAN
Three interactions
Moore (1989); Hillman et al. (1994)
  • Learner ↔ Content. The reading, the video, the case, the Content block itself.
  • Learner ↔ Instructor. Welcomes, feedback touchpoints, scripted instructor presence.
  • Learner ↔ Learner. Peer discussion, peer review, collaborative interpretation.
  • Learner ↔ AI. Guided prompt practice, AI-coached writing, simulated patients.
GARRISON ET AL.
Three presences
Garrison et al. (2000)
  • Teaching presence. Design, facilitation, and direct instruction from the instructor.
  • Social presence. Learners projecting themselves authentically into the cohort.
  • Cognitive presence. Sustained inquiry that moves from trigger to resolution.
THE DESIGN TEST
An activity that moves only learner-content interaction activates at best cognitive presence. An activity that moves all four interactions has a reasonable chance of activating all three presences at once. That is the quiet rubric behind every design choice in this unit.
4.3 Process · Activity Design Loop

The six-step design loop

This is the loop you run every time you design or redesign a DE activity with AI. Click each step to see what it asks and what a faculty member would actually write.

Outcome

Name one specific learning outcome this activity serves. Every downstream decision follows from it.
An AI-enhanced activity without a stated outcome will drift toward whatever the model generates well. The outcome anchors the design and tells you when the activity is done.
QUESTIONS TO ANSWER
  • What is the single learning outcome this activity serves?
  • Is the outcome measurable in a DE context?
  • Does the outcome require AI, or would a standard activity serve it?

4.4 Tabs · Four Activity Patterns

Four patterns that work in healthcare DE

Four designs that consistently reach Modification or Redefinition on SAMR in healthcare education. Click any pattern to see its mechanics, its SAMR tier, and the presences it activates.

Case Debate with AI Devil's Advocate

Two learner groups take a clinical position. The AI argues the opposing case at equal strength.

Learners in a small group take a clinical position on a case (e.g., anticoagulate this patient now). A second group takes the opposing position. Each group then prompts an AI to produce the strongest counterargument against their own position, forcing them to confront the other side on its best terms.

Groups then debate in a synchronous session or an asynchronous discussion thread. The AI's role is adversarial collaborator, not answer key. Learners cite the AI's counterargument in their final reasoning artifact, including whether it changed their mind.

WHY IT CLEARS MODIFICATION Without AI, the exercise would require two instructors to role-play opposing clinicians. The activity was impractical at scale. AI makes it routine, and the quality of the counterargument raises the cognitive demand on both sides of the debate.
SAMR TIER
MModification
Task redesign that was previously impractical at scale.
INTERACTIONS (MOORE)
All four
Learner-content, learner-instructor, learner-learner, learner-AI.
COI PRESENCES
All three
Teaching (design + closure), social (group debate), cognitive (sustained inquiry).
BEST FIT
Clinical reasoning courses
Pharmacotherapy, medical decision-making, nursing critical care.

AI-Coached Writing

AI functions as a writing coach during drafting, never as a co-author.

Learners draft scholarly writing (a clinical reflection, a case analysis, a quality improvement proposal). At specified checkpoints, they submit their draft to an AI coach that asks questions about the writing rather than rewriting it — questions about argument structure, evidence, clarity, and alignment with the rubric.

The AI's output is a set of coaching questions, not edits. Learners then revise in response to the questions and document how their thinking changed. Final submission includes the original draft, the coaching dialogue, and the revised version.

WHY IT CLEARS MODIFICATION Traditional peer review in DE is asymmetric — some learners get thoughtful reviews, others do not. AI coaching gives every learner the same thoughtful questioning on their schedule, while preserving the revision work as the learner's own.
SAMR TIER
MModification
Scales expert questioning previously impossible to distribute evenly.
INTERACTIONS (MOORE)
Three of four
Learner-content, learner-instructor (at checkpoints), learner-AI.
COI PRESENCES
Teaching + cognitive
Social presence requires pairing with peer exchange.
BEST FIT
Writing-heavy courses
Reflection-based clinical courses, QI/research writing, capstone preparation.

AI Standardized Patient

Learners interview an AI patient, debrief with peers, and receive faculty feedback.

Replaces or supplements expensive standardized patient encounters in DE. An AI is prompted to play a patient with a specific clinical presentation, history, and communication style. Learners conduct the interview asynchronously, taking time to consider each question, while the AI holds the patient role consistently.

After the interview, learners write a clinical note, post the note and interview transcript to a peer discussion, and receive faculty-led debrief feedback on interviewing technique, clinical reasoning, and communication. The activity closes synchronously with a cohort debrief.

WHY IT CLEARS REDEFINITION This activity did not exist before reliable generative AI. Asynchronous standardized-patient practice at scale was not feasible in DE. The combination of individualized interview practice, peer review of the transcript, and faculty-led debrief is a learning experience no prior format could offer.
SAMR TIER
RRedefinition
A task previously inconceivable in asynchronous DE.
INTERACTIONS (MOORE)
All four
Content, instructor, peer, AI — each at a different phase.
COI PRESENCES
All three
Teaching (debrief), social (peer review), cognitive (sustained clinical reasoning).
BEST FIT
Clinical communication courses
History-taking, motivational interviewing, difficult conversations, cultural competence.

AI-Annotated Reading

Learners read a guideline in one pane. AI provides clarifying commentary in an adjacent pane.

Learners receive a dense clinical reading (a guideline, a systematic review, a policy brief). Alongside it, they interact with an AI that can explain, contextualize, or challenge any passage they highlight — but only when prompted, and only within constraints the instructor has scripted.

Learners submit their annotation trail: which passages they queried, what they asked, what the AI said, and what they concluded after integrating the AI response with their own thinking. The annotations become a record of the learner's engagement with the reading.

WHY IT CLEARS MODIFICATION A graduate student reading a guideline alone cannot easily ask a follow-up question. An AI co-reader makes structured interrogation of dense material routine, and makes the learner's reading process visible to the instructor for the first time.
SAMR TIER
MModification
Makes visible what was previously private (the reading process itself).
INTERACTIONS (MOORE)
Three of four
Learner-content, learner-AI, optional learner-learner.
COI PRESENCES
Cognitive + teaching
Social presence optional via shared annotation.
BEST FIT
Evidence-based practice
Journal clubs, guideline analysis, policy interpretation, literature reviews.

4.7 Labeled Graphic · Anatomy of Redefinition

Anatomy of a Redefinition activity

Six elements that a Redefinition-tier activity reliably carries. Hover or click any card to highlight it. Use this as a checklist against your own canvas in Block 4.8.

ANNOTATED EXAMPLE · AI STANDARDIZED PATIENT
A 90-minute asynchronous clinical interview, peer review, and synchronous debrief
This activity could not exist before reliable generative AI. Every element below is non-negotiable; remove one and the activity drops to Modification or lower.
ELEMENT 01 O
Single learning outcome
Named explicitly. "Conduct a motivational interview with a patient considering smoking cessation, producing a clinical note." No ambiguity about what learners should leave able to do.
ELEMENT 02 AI
Specific AI role
Bounded and scripted. The AI holds one patient persona consistently across the interview. It is not a generic chatbot. The faculty member wrote and tested the system prompt before deployment.
ELEMENT 03 ↔
All four interactions
Sequenced across the activity. Learner-AI (interview), learner-content (note writing), learner-learner (peer review of transcripts), learner-instructor (cohort debrief).
ELEMENT 04 P
All three presences
Distributed by design. Teaching presence in the opening brief and the debrief. Social presence in the peer review phase. Cognitive presence sustained through the interview and the clinical note.
ELEMENT 05 R
Self-regulation cue
Goal-setting at start, reflection at end. Learners set one interview goal before they begin. Post-debrief, they write a 200-word reflection on what the AI patient revealed about their own technique.
ELEMENT 06 =
Criterion-referenced assessment
Rubric aligned to the outcome. The interview transcript, the clinical note, and the reflection are assessed against a rubric that measures interview technique, clinical reasoning, and metacognitive awareness. AI use is part of the rubric, not outside it.
