2.2 Text on Image · Reframe

A prompt is a lesson plan

Most faculty treat AI prompts as commands. That framing produces command-grade output. Reframe the prompt as an instructional artifact and the output changes.

Stop writing commands.
Start writing lesson plans the model can execute.
WHY THIS REFRAME MATTERS

A command describes an action. A lesson plan describes an outcome, the learner, the conditions, and the constraints. When you prompt an LLM, you are handing it a lesson plan for a one-time task. The quality of your instruction determines the quality of its work.

This is also how Technology Acceptance Model predicts adoption. Perceived usefulness rises sharply when faculty see AI producing pedagogically useful output — which is what happens when prompts are written like plans.

2.3 Process · The RCTFC Model

The five-part prompt

Click each letter to see its role, its purpose, and a clinical education example. Work through all five to complete the block.

Role

Name the expertise and stance the model should adopt before it produces anything.
A model without a role defaults to generic, average-case output. A model with a role draws from a specific slice of its training. Role-setting is the single highest-leverage edit most faculty can make.
CLINICAL EDUCATION EXAMPLE
"You are an expert health science instructor in pharmacotherapy with ten years of teaching experience in asynchronous DE formats..."
  • Names an expertise area (not just "a helpful assistant").
  • Gives a stance the model can hold across the whole prompt.
  • Bounds the domain — pharmacotherapy, not all of pharmacy.
2.4 Tabs · Weak vs Strong

Three side-by-side diagnostics

The same instructional task prompted two ways. Read both prompts and both outputs. Notice what the RCTFC structure changes.

Pharmacy faculty needs a case-based scenario for a Week 4 cardiology discussion board — asynchronous DE, graduate level.
Weak Command-grade
PROMPT
"Write a pharmacy case about heart failure."
OUTPUT
A 58-year-old man presents with shortness of breath and fatigue. PMH: hypertension, diabetes. Discuss management. [Generic. No clinical depth. No teaching signal. Requires full rewrite.]
Strong RCTFC-grade
PROMPT
"You are a pharmacotherapy instructor [R]. My Week 4 cardiology module is for graduate pharmacy students in an asynchronous DE course [C]. Draft one case-based scenario for a discussion board [T]. Format: 200-word vignette plus three guiding questions [F]. Avoid demographic stereotyping, include one clinical ambiguity, cite your reasoning at the end [Constraints]."
OUTPUT
A 62-year-old patient presents to ambulatory care with worsening dyspnea on exertion, orthopnea, and a 3 kg weight gain over two weeks. Current regimen: lisinopril 20mg, metoprolol succinate 50mg, furosemide 40mg daily. The ambiguity: recent start of NSAID for joint pain. [Usable. Teaching signal clear. Ambiguity invites discussion.]
Nursing faculty needs a scaffolded summary of a dense clinical guideline to use as a pre-reading in an online course.
Weak Command-grade
PROMPT
"Summarize this sepsis guideline."
OUTPUT
The guideline covers sepsis recognition, early antibiotic administration, and fluid resuscitation. [Flat list. No scaffolding. No learner fit. Doesn't match reading level of new DE learners.]
Strong RCTFC-grade
PROMPT
"You are an academic writing coach for nursing learners [R]. My learner is a clinician new to asynchronous DE coursework [C]. Convert this sepsis guideline into a scaffolded study guide [T]. Format: three tiers — Essential, Advanced, Optional [F]. Use plain language, preserve APA citations, flag content requiring verification [Constraints]."
OUTPUT
ESSENTIAL: Sepsis is a life-threatening response to infection. Recognize early (qSOFA, lactate). Start broad-spectrum antibiotics within 1 hour. ADVANCED: Source control, resuscitation targets, vasopressor choice. OPTIONAL: Biomarker debates, post-sepsis syndrome. [Tiered. Learner-ready. Verification flags included.]
Health science faculty needs three discussion questions that activate cognitive presence for a weekly reading.
Weak Command-grade
PROMPT
"Give me discussion questions about this article."
OUTPUT
1. What did you learn? 2. What surprised you? 3. Do you agree? [Generic. Low cognitive demand. Does not drive CoI inquiry.]
Strong RCTFC-grade
PROMPT
"You are a DE course designer [R]. Learners are graduate health science students in a CoI-framed course [C]. Draft three discussion questions that activate cognitive presence per Garrison et al. (2000) [T]. Format: one triggering, one exploratory, one integrative [F]. Each requires evidence from the reading; avoid single-right-answer framing [Constraints]."
OUTPUT
TRIGGERING: Where does the author's position conflict with your current clinical practice? EXPLORATORY: What evidence in the reading would change a clinician's decision-making at the bedside? INTEGRATIVE: How would you reconcile this with the competing framework covered in Week 2? [Phase-specific. CoI-aligned. Evidence-grounded.]

R HOTSPOT 01
Role
Bounded expertise and stance. Pharmacotherapy, not all pharmacy. DE-experienced, not generic.
C HOTSPOT 02
Context
Course, module, audience, modality, and aligned learning outcome. Every variable named.
T HOTSPOT 03
Task
A single verb-driven, measurable instructional action. One output, clearly bounded.
F HOTSPOT 04
Format
Output structure tied to instructional use. Length and shape both specified.
C HOTSPOT 05
Constraints
Ethics, accuracy, and scope guardrails. Prevents generic or harmful output.
